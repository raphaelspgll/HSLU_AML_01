Missing Data Analysis

Explanation: Why Are Some Variables Mostly NA?

The high NA rates for certain variables are structural and expected – they are not the result of a merge error. According to the HEAPO dataset paper (Brudermueller et al., 2025):

Variable NA rate Reason
kWh_heatpump, kWh_other ~97% Only available for households with a separate smart meter for the heat pump.
kWh_returned ~75% Only available for households with a PV system (~25% of households).
sunshine_hours ~8% Not all 8 weather stations measure sunshine duration.
precipitation ~0.2% Minor gaps in weather station data.

Implication for modelling: kWh_total is the primary response variable. Since heat pumps are the dominant electricity consumer, kWh_total serves as a valid proxy for heat pump energy consumption.

NA Pattern: Structural or Random?

What we want to know: Is the missingness in kWh_heatpump random (a data quality problem) or structural (the household simply has no sub-meter)?

What to look for: If a household is either always missing or never missing across its entire observation period, the missingness is structural – the sub-meter is either installed or it is not. Random missingness would show a mix within the same household over time.

missing_per_hh <- heapo_daily %>%
  group_by(Household_ID) %>%
  summarise(pct_missing = mean(is.na(kWh_heatpump)) * 100, .groups = "drop") %>%
  mutate(
    Category = case_when(
      pct_missing == 100 ~ "Always missing (no sub-meter)",
      pct_missing == 0 ~ "Never missing (has sub-meter)",
      TRUE ~ "Partially missing (unusual)"
    )
  )

missing_per_hh %>%
  count(Category) %>%
  mutate(Percent = round(n / sum(n) * 100, 1)) %>%

Conclusion: Almost all households fall into one of the two extreme categories – either always missing or never missing. This confirms that the ~97% NA rate is structural (no sub-meter installed), not a data quality problem. Any households in the “Partially missing” category would warrant further investigation.

Complete Coverage Analysis: Full Period vs. From 2022

Since the daily dataset contains too many observations for certain modelling methods (NN, SVM require at most ~100,000 rows), we need to define a usable subset. A key question is: how many households have complete and uninterrupted data over a given time window?

What we want to know: How many households can we include while staying under 100,000 observations, without introducing gaps or bias from incomplete coverage?

start_2022 <- as.Date("2022-01-01")
days_full <- as.numeric(max(heapo_daily$date) - min(heapo_daily$date))
days_2022 <- as.numeric(max(heapo_daily$date) - start_2022)

# Full period coverage per household
cov_full <- heapo_daily %>%
  group_by(Household_ID) %>%
  summarise(n_days = n(), .groups = "drop") %>%
  mutate(coverage_pct = n_days / days_full * 100)

# From 2022 coverage per household
cov_2022 <- heapo_daily %>%
  filter(date >= start_2022) %>%
  group_by(Household_ID) %>%
  summarise(n_days = n(), .groups = "drop") %>%
  mutate(coverage_pct = n_days / days_2022 * 100)

tibble(

Recommended Subset for Modelling (Daily Dataset)

Given the 100,000 observation limit required for methods such as Neural Networks and SVMs, the recommended subset is defined as follows:

  • Time window: 2022-01-01 to 2024-02-29
  • Household selection: Randomly sample from households with >=95% coverage in this window
  • Maximum households: 126 (stays within the 100,000 row limit)
complete_2022 <- cov_2022 %>%
  filter(coverage_pct >= 95) %>%
  arrange(desc(n_days))

cumulative_rows <- cumsum(complete_2022$n_days)
max_hh <- sum(cumulative_rows <= 100000)
actual_rows <- cumulative_rows[max_hh]

tibble(

    as.character(round(mean(complete_2022$n_days[1:max_hh])))
  )
) %>%

This subset covers two full years including all seasons, ensuring seasonal patterns (winter heating peaks, summer lows) are fully represented. The random sampling from 403 eligible households avoids selection bias.

Why kWh_total Works as a Response Variable

Although most households have no separate heat pump meter, kWh_total is a valid response variable for three reasons:

  1. Heat pumps dominate consumption: In Swiss households with heat pumps as primary heating, the heat pump accounts for 50-80% of total electricity use. Variation in kWh_total is therefore largely driven by heat pump behaviour.
  2. Predictors explain heating demand: Variables such as heating_degree_days, living_area, heatpump_type, and has_floor_heating directly capture factors that drive heat pump operation.
  3. Validation is possible: For the ~2.5% of households with sub-metering available, kWh_heatpump can be used to verify that model results correctly attribute variation to heat pump consumption.
